TYPE OF ENTITIES IN DATABASE DESIGN

1. STRONG ENTITY (Independent Entity)
A strong entity is an entity that can exist independently and has its own primary key. It doesn't depend on any other entity for its existence.

Characteristics:
- Has its own primary key
- Can exist without other entities
- Represented by single rectangle in ER diagram
- Uniquely identified by primary key alone

Example:
Customer(CustomerID, CustomerName, Email, Phone)
- CustomerID is the primary key
- Customer can exist independently
- Doesn't need another entity to define it

Another Example:
Employee(EmployeeID, EmployeeName, Address, Salary)
- EmployeeID is primary key
- Employee exists independently

2. WEAK ENTITY (Dependent Entity)
A weak entity is an entity that cannot exist independently. It depends on a strong entity for its existence and uses a partial key combined with the strong entity's primary key to uniquely identify itself.

Characteristics:
- Does NOT have its own primary key
- Depends on another entity for existence
- Has a partial key (discriminator)
- Represented by double rectangle in ER diagram
- Needs foreign key + partial key to uniquely identify

Example:
Student has DependentFamily members
Strong Entity: Student(StudentID, StudentName, Address)
Weak Entity: Dependent(DependentID, DependentName, Relationship)
- DependentID alone is not unique across all dependents
- StudentID + DependentID together form composite primary key
- If student is deleted, their dependents must also be deleted (referential integrity)

Another Example:
Hotel and Room
Strong Entity: Hotel(HotelID, HotelName, Location, Rating)
Weak Entity: Room(RoomNumber, RoomType, Price)
- RoomNumber is not unique globally (every hotel has Room 101)
- HotelID + RoomNumber together uniquely identify a room
- If hotel is deleted, all rooms are deleted

Another Example:
Course and CourseSection
Strong Entity: Course(CourseID, CourseName, Credits)
Weak Entity: CourseSection(SectionID, Semester, Instructor, TimeSlot)
- SectionID alone doesn't identify section (multiple courses have Section 1)
- CourseID + SectionID uniquely identifies section

3. ASSOCIATIVE ENTITY (Junction/Bridge Entity)
An associative entity is created to resolve many-to-many relationships. It contains the primary keys of both related entities as foreign keys and may have its own additional attributes.

Characteristics:
- Represents a many-to-many relationship
- Contains at least two foreign keys from related entities
- Can have additional attributes describing the relationship
- Primary key is usually composite of both foreign keys
- Can be represented as entity or as relationship with attributes

Example:
Student enrolls in Course (many-to-many relationship)
Student(StudentID, StudentName, Email)
Course(CourseID, CourseName, Credits)
Enrollment(StudentID, CourseID, EnrollmentDate, Grade, Status)
- Enrollment is associative entity
- StudentID and CourseID are foreign keys
- EnrollmentDate and Grade are additional attributes
- Primary key: (StudentID, CourseID)

Another Example:
Employee works on Project (many-to-many)
Employee(EmployeeID, EmployeeName, Salary)
Project(ProjectID, ProjectName, Budget)
Assignment(EmployeeID, ProjectID, HoursWorked, Role, StartDate)
- Assignment is associative entity
- EmployeeID and ProjectID are foreign keys
- HoursWorked and Role describe the relationship
- Primary key: (EmployeeID, ProjectID)

Another Example:
Doctor and Patient (many-to-many)
Doctor(DoctorID, DoctorName, Specialization)
Patient(PatientID, PatientName, Age)
Consultation(DoctorID, PatientID, ConsultationDate, Diagnosis, Treatment)
- Consultation is associative entity

4. REGULAR ENTITY (Simple Entity)
An entity that has a single attribute as primary key and represents a single object type.

Example:
Book(BookID, Title, Author, PublishYear)
Department(DepartmentID, DepartmentName, Location)

5. COMPOSITE ENTITY
An entity that consists of multiple components or attributes that together form the entity.

Example:
Address could be composite: Street + City + State + ZipCode
Name could be composite: FirstName + LastName + MiddleName

In database: Person(PersonID, FirstName, LastName, MiddleName, Street, City, State, ZipCode)


COMPARISON TABLE: STRONG vs WEAK ENTITY

Feature                 Strong Entity           Weak Entity
Primary Key             Has its own PK          No own PK, partial key only
Independence            Can exist alone         Depends on strong entity
Identification          PK alone identifies     PK + FK identifies
ER Diagram              Single rectangle        Double rectangle
Relationship            One-to-Many            One-to-One/One-to-Many
Deletion                Can be deleted          Deleted when parent deleted
Example                 Customer, Employee      Dependent, Room


REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE: COLLEGE DATABASE

Strong Entities:
1. Student(StudentID, StudentName, Email, Phone, Address)
2. Course(CourseID, CourseName, Credits, Department)
3. Instructor(InstructorID, InstructorName, Department, Specialization)
4. Department(DepartmentID, DepartmentName, Location)

Weak Entities:
1. CourseSection(CourseID, SectionID, Semester, Instructor, TimeSlot)
   - Depends on Course
   - SectionID alone doesn't identify it globally
   - Composite PK: (CourseID, SectionID)

2. StudentContact(StudentID, ContactID, ContactName, PhoneNumber, Relationship)
   - Depends on Student
   - Composite PK: (StudentID, ContactID)

Associative Entities:
1. Enrollment(StudentID, CourseID, SectionID, EnrollmentDate, Grade, Status)
   - Represents many-to-many between Student and Course
   - Composite PK: (StudentID, CourseID, SectionID)

2. Prerequisite(CourseID, PrerequisiteCourseID, Minimum Grade)
   - Represents many-to-many within Course (course prerequisites)
   - Composite PK: (CourseID, PrerequisiteCourseID)


ANOTHER EXAMPLE: HOSPITAL DATABASE

Strong Entities:
1. Patient(PatientID, PatientName, DateOfBirth, Address, Phone)
2. Doctor(DoctorID, DoctorName, Specialization, Qualification)
3. Department(DepartmentID, DepartmentName, Location, HeadDoctor)
4. Medicine(MedicineID, MedicineName, Manufacturer, Price)

Weak Entities:
1. PatientAdmission(PatientID, AdmissionID, AdmissionDate, DischargeDate, WardNumber)
   - Depends on Patient
   - Cannot exist without patient
   - Composite PK: (PatientID, AdmissionID)

2. MedicalRecord(PatientID, RecordID, RecordDate, Diagnosis, Treatment)
   - Depends on Patient
   - Composite PK: (PatientID, RecordID)

Associative Entities:
1. Consultation(PatientID, DoctorID, ConsultationDate, ConsultationTime, Diagnosis, Fee)
   - Many-to-many between Patient and Doctor
   - Composite PK: (PatientID, DoctorID, ConsultationDate)

2. Prescription(PatientID, MedicineID, ConsultationDate, Dosage, Frequency, Duration)
   - Many-to-many between Patient and Medicine
   - Composite PK: (PatientID, MedicineID, ConsultationDate)


KEY POINTS TO REMEMBER

1. Strong entities have primary keys and can exist independently
2. Weak entities lack primary keys and depend on strong entities
3. Associative entities resolve many-to-many relationships
4. Weak entity primary key = Strong entity PK + Weak entity partial key
5. Use double rectangle for weak entities in ER diagrams
6. Weak entities follow referential integrity (cascade delete)
7. Associative entities contain at least 2 foreign keys
8. Many-to-many relationships must be converted to associative entities in relational model

IDENTIFYING ENTITY TYPE - CHECKLIST

To determine if entity is Strong/Weak/Associative:

1. Does it have its own unique primary key?
   - YES = Strong Entity (unless it's resolving M:N)
   - NO = Weak Entity

2. Can it exist without another entity?
   - YES = Strong Entity
   - NO = Weak Entity

3. Is it created to resolve a many-to-many relationship?
   - YES = Associative Entity
   - NO = Continue checking

4. Does it contain multiple foreign keys?
   - YES = Likely Associative Entity
   - NO = Likely Regular Entity

5. Does its existence depend on another entity?
   - YES = Weak Entity
   - NO = Strong Entity

This checklist helps quickly identify entity types in any database design problem.
